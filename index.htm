Introduction

On the surface, plug-in battery electric vehicles (EV) seem like the natural progression of logistics technology. The idea of never again having to buy gasoline, never again having to pay for a tune-up, never again having to worry if your car will start this morning because of the snow and ice… it almost sounds too good to be true. Cynics and skeptics decry these statements as overly simplistic, doubting the claims made by the automotive companies producing the vehicles and dismissing reports of gargantuan savings from their owners as being the result of the early part of the hype cycle [1]. But as with most truly hard problems, it's not even as simple as the cynic and skeptic make it out to be.

Analysis of the full life cycle of EVs—from the mining of resources such as lithium to the disposal of the same resources after the vehicle is no longer usable—reveals that significant pollution and toxic chemicals are released over the course of the vehicle's full existence [2, 3]. However, the production of conventional vehicles (CV) is equally damaging, with estimates putting the carbon footprint of producing a single new vehicle at almost the same amount as the carbon released from powering a typical home in the UK for three years [4]. Analyzing the production processes of EVs and CVs to determine which is more damaging asks a different question than is asked in this study, and as such the naïve approach has been taken, comparing only the costs the consumer faces—the initial purchase price, the cost of fuel, and the cost of scheduled maintenance—and fixing the pre- and post-consumer costs as being essentially equivalent, reserving the true analysis thereof for another day.

Worst Case Scenario

Without a doubt, a comparable EV is more expensive than its CV counterpart, and this is almost completely due to the batteries present in the EV. Data on the topic of vehicle maintenance costs are routinely collected by the consumer advocacy group Consumer Reports, which surveys thousands of their subscribers every year about the reliability and maintenance requirements of their vehicles and appliances [5]. Maintenance cost reduction, while significant, is not the main benefit that is touted with regard to EV adoption, and is likely not significantly responsible for the rapid growth of the non-commercial EV fleet, even if it plays a more significant part in EV adoption in the commercial sector. Fuel cost savings are what is generally regarded as the single greatest benefit that EV have over CV. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) estimates the average combined miles per gallon (MPG) of the base model 2021 Nissan LEAF to be 111 MGP equivalent [6], whereas the Nissan Sentra from the same year received an estimated average combined MPG rating of 33 [7]. The estimated average annual fuel cost, which uses a value of 15,000 miles as the distance traveled in one year, is given as $600 and $1,450 for the LEAF and Sentra, respectively [8].
