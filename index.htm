Introducing The Question

On the surface, plug-in electric vehicles (PEV) seem like the natural progression of logistics technology. The idea of never again having to buy gasoline, never again having to pay for a tune-up, never again having to worry if your car will start this morning because of the snow and ice… it almost sounds too good to be true. Cynics and skeptics decry these statements as overly simplistic, doubting the claims made by the automotive companies producing the vehicles and dismissing reports from users as being the result of the early part of the hype cycle [1]. But as with most truly hard problems, it's not even as simple as the cynic and skeptic make it out to be.

Analysis of the full lifecycle of PEVs—from the mining of resources such as lithium to the disposal of the same resources after the vehicle is no longer usable—reveals that significant pollution and toxic chemicals are released over the course of the vehicle's full lifecycle [2, 3]. However, previous comparisons of this more realistic calculation to the economic and environmental cost of purchasing a conventional internal combustion engive vehicle (ICEV) have often either left off the same scrutiny for the ICEV, or have manipulated the input values for their model so as to make the PEV appear worse for the environment [3, 4, 5]. This issue is only exacerbated by popular science media, who often take a publication at face value without giving critical investigation to the methodologies used in the research [3, 5].
